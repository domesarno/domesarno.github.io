Comparative Analysis and Mathematical Properties

Analogy of Two Simulations

The two homework assignments—one demonstrating the Law of Large Numbers (LLN) and the other simulating server security to show convergence to the Binomial Distribution—are both powerful examples of Monte Carlo simulation built upon the same foundation: the Bernoulli process.

A Bernoulli process is a sequence of independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables, where each trial has exactly two possible outcomes (e.g., success/failure, heads/tails, secure/breached). Both simulations explore the collective behavior of these trials as the number of trials, $n$, increases.

Core Similarities

Despite their different goals, the simulations share a common architecture:

  • Stochastic Foundation: Both model phenomena as a sequence of $n$ independent random trials. The outcome of one trial (or week) has no influence on the next.
  • Bernoulli Trial: The LLN simulation (HW4) uses a simple, *fair* Bernoulli trial (outcome 0 or 1, $p=0.5$). The Server simulation (HW7) uses a *compound*, *biased* Bernoulli trial (outcome -1 or +1), where the probability $P_{\text{secure}} = (1-p)^m$ is itself derived from $m$ other trials, but remains a constant probability for each week.
  • Monte Carlo Method: Both run a large number of independent "trajectories" ($m$ or `num_sims`) to show how the empirical, simulated results converge to the theoretical, mathematical truth.

Fundamental Differences

The primary difference lies in *what* is being measured and *what principle* is being demonstrated.

  • What is Measured?
    • The LLN (HW4) simulation measures the running average of the outcomes ($\frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$) *within* each trajectory as $n$ increases.
    • The Server (HW7) simulation measures the final sum (or score, $2k-n$) *across* all trajectories for a fixed $n$.
  • What is Demonstrated?
    • LLN (HW4) demonstrates that the sample mean converges to the true mean ($E[X]$) as $n \to \infty$. It's a statement about the convergence of a single value.
    • Server (HW7) demonstrates that the distribution of the sum of $n$ trials conforms to the theoretical Binomial Distribution. It's a statement about the *shape* of the resulting probability mass function.
  • The Random Variable:
    • In LLN (HW4), the variable $X_i$ is $\{0, 1\}$. We track its average.
    • In Server (HW7), the variable $X_i$ is $\{-1, +1\}$, which is the definition of a Random Walk. We track the distribution of its final position.

Relationship to Binomial Coefficients

The core of the Server simulation (HW7) is built on binomial coefficients. A binomial coefficient, written as $\binom{n}{k}$, counts the number of ways to choose $k$ items from a set of $n$ (e.g., "n choose k").

In our Server simulation, a final score of $2k-n$ is achieved by having exactly $k$ "secure" weeks and $n-k$ "breach" weeks. The number of different trajectories (or paths) that can lead to this exact outcome is precisely $\binom{n}{k}$.

This is why the theoretical probability is (Number of Paths) $\times$ (Probability of one specific path):

$$ P(\text{Score} = 2k-n) = P(k \text{ successes}) = \binom{n}{k} (P_{\text{secure}})^k (P_{\text{breach}})^{n-k} $$

Pascal's Triangle and Random Walks

Pascal's Triangle is the geometric arrangement of binomial coefficients. Each number in the triangle is the sum of the two numbers directly above it.

$$ \binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k} $$
[Image of Pascal's Triangle]

This is not just a mathematical curiosity; it is the exact formula for a simple random walk (where $p=0.5$). The number of distinct paths a walker can take from the origin to a position $j$ after $n$ steps is a number found in the triangle.

The rows of Pascal's Triangle *are* the probability distributions for a simple random walk (like flipping a fair coin). Our Server simulation is a *biased* random walk, but the underlying combinatorial structure (the counting of paths) is the same, and it's described by Pascal's Triangle.

The Binomial Expansion

The Binomial Theorem states how to expand a binomial $(x+y)$ raised to a power $n$:

$$ (x+y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} x^k y^{n-k} $$

This is the generating function for the binomial distribution. If we set $x = P_{\text{secure}}$ and $y = P_{\text{breach}}$, the expansion becomes:

$$ (P_{\text{secure}} + P_{\text{breach}})^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} (P_{\text{secure}})^k (P_{\text{breach}})^{n-k} $$

Since $P_{\text{secure}} + P_{\text{breach}} = 1$, the left side is $1^n = 1$. The right side is the sum of all possible outcomes (from $k=0$ secure weeks to $k=n$ secure weeks). Each individual term in that sum is precisely the binomial probability $P(K=k)$ that we plotted in our simulation.

Combinatorics and the Fibonacci Sequence

While the Fibonacci sequence ($1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, \dots$) is not directly modeled in our simulations, it is deeply related to the combinatorial coefficients we've been using.

The $n$-th Fibonacci number, $F_n$, can be expressed as the sum of the "shallow diagonals" of Pascal's Triangle.

$$ F_n = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor (n-1)/2 \rfloor} \binom{n-1-k}{k} $$

This formula arises from a classic combinatorial problem: $F_n$ represents the number of ways to tile a $1 \times (n-1)$ board using $1 \times 1$ squares and $1 \times 2$ dominoes. This illustrates a broader principle: binomial coefficients (and by extension, Pascal's Triangle) are a fundamental 'lookup table' for combinatorial problems, appearing in everything from probability distributions to random walks and even sequence generation.
