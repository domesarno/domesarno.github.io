Research Task – Measures of Location and Dispersion

Introduction

In statistics, measures of location (or central tendency) and dispersion (or variability) are fundamental to summarize and describe datasets. Measures of location identify the "center" or typical value of a dataset, while measures of dispersion describe how spread out the data are around that center.

Measures of Location (Central Tendency)

The most common measures of central tendency are:

  • Arithmetic Mean: The sum of all observations divided by their number. It is sensitive to extreme values (outliers). Formula: mean = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ) / n
  • Median: The middle value of an ordered dataset. It is more robust to outliers and skewed distributions.
  • Mode: The value that occurs most frequently in a dataset. It is particularly useful for categorical or discrete data.

Measures of Dispersion

Measures of dispersion describe the degree to which data values differ from the mean or median. Common examples include:

  • Variance (σ²): The average of the squared deviations from the mean. Formula: σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / n
  • Standard Deviation (σ): The square root of the variance. It expresses the average distance from the mean in the same units as the data.
  • Interquartile Range (IQR): The difference between the 75th and 25th percentiles (Q₃ − Q₁), representing the range of the middle 50% of the data.

Applications, Advantages, and Limitations

Measures of location and dispersion are widely used in descriptive and inferential statistics. Means are used when data are symmetrically distributed, while medians are preferred for skewed data or in the presence of outliers. Variance and standard deviation provide information about the consistency or variability of data, useful in finance, manufacturing, and scientific experiments.

However, these measures have limitations: the mean is influenced by outliers, variance and standard deviation assume interval or ratio scales, and the mode may be unstable when multiple peaks occur.
